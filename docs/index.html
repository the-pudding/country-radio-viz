Simulations

It’s 8:35am on a Friday morning. You hop into your car and turn on the local country radio station. A classic: Brooks & Dunn’s “That Ain’t No Way To Go.”

You’ve got nothing else to do today but ride around and blast some songs. After a few hours you hit a stretch of Keith Urban, Kenny Chesney, Michael Ray, Tim McGraw, Thomas Rhett, Easton Corbin, Chris Stapleton, Sam Hunt, George Strait, Jordan Davis, Cody Johnson, and Jon Pardi all back-to-back-to-back.

Noticing a trend here? All men. In fact, here are all the back-to-back songs by men that have been played since you started listening.

It’ll be another 9 hours and 16 minutes before you hear a rarity on country radio: back-to-back songs by women artists.

If you listened to the radio non-stop from 12am to 12pm today, you’d likely only hear 3 back-to-back songs by women, compared to 245 from men.

Songs by women are already severely underplayed, accounting for around 10% of total spins. Left up to chance we should see 30-40 back-to-back plays each day by women, like in these simulations. In reality, we see 3.

And it’s not just this one “bad” day (which happens to be an actual day: January 7, 2022).

We looked at 19 dates throughout 2022 for this same radio station, and found that out of 6,474 songs only 64 (0.99%) were back-to-back women plays. For men, it was 4,231 (65.53%). Mixed-gender ensembles and collaborations account for 36 (0.56%) songs.

To pick the dates, we started with the last Friday of each month, and then added 7 more release dates for top-selling women country artists, hoping to see back-to-back plays for women jump. They didn’t. On average, 4 back-to-back women’s songs are played on release days compared to 3 songs on “regular” Fridays.

It’s even bad when you recode mixed-gender collaborations and mixed-gender ensembles as women, like some radio stations do. Counted like this, women’s songs account for 200 (3.1%) back-to-back plays, roughly 10 per day, still far below the 30-40 plays we’d expect.

So what might explain this? Let’s start with Tomatogate.

Back in 2015, a radio consultant said in an interview about programming that women in country music are not the lettuce in the salad, but the tomatoes — they should be “sprinkled” on the playlist and not played back-to-back.

And long before that, the rule about avoiding playing women back-to-back was even written into a radio manual:

“It’s important to orient the announcers about the general preferences I have about music flow, the formatting rules I want them to follow. [...] For example, I don’t want more than two ballads in a row. I want to avoid having more than two female singers in a row.”

Knowing this existing gender bias in country radio, we set out to find out just how bad is bad. The answer: HORRENDOUS.

We looked at 29 country radio stations across the US — both large markets and large country markets. The station data we saw earlier was from San Antonio’s KCYY-FM, and it’s actually one of the better stations for back-to-back women plays at 0.99%.

When women are played back-to-back it often comes with caveats.

First, they’re often played during the evening (7pm–midnight) and overnight (midnight–6am) hours when listenership is down. On average 64% percent of back-to-back plays for songs by women come during these hours. Very “if women artists are played back-to-back and no one is around to hear it, does it make a sound?” energy.

A good chunk of women’s back-to-back plays are also “gold” songs, or songs that were big hits, but not current — think Carrie Underwood or Taylor Swift circa 2005. An average of 40% of women back-to-back plays are gold songs, meaning that there’s reduced room for today’s biggest women country stars like Maren Morris, Miranda Lambert, and Lainey Wilson, who are among 2022’s most-played artists.

Now, no one’s hating on Carrie or Taylor — their classics deserve lasting airplay — but it does seem like country radio largely avoids today’s women’s hits.

And when current songs do get played, it’s often the same handful of artists. If you took out the top 3 current women artists you’d be left with just x% of songs, compared to X% if you did the same for men.

So women have the salad stacked against them: they are played less often overall, less often back-to-back, less often during prime listening hours, and less often even if they have new music.

If women are this poorly represented on country radio day-to-day, that means that artists of color, women artists of color, queer artists, and those at all the intersections are even worse off. Non-binary and trans artists are nonexistent on mainstream country radio.

Where back-to-back plays by women were on average 0.50% of all plays, back-to-back plays by all artists of color averaged 0.41% and back-to-back plays by women artists of color were non-existent.

So not only is country radio not playing the “tomatoes” of the salad, it’s not playing the cucumbers, or the carrots, or the celery, or the croutons, or even the dressing. You’ve just got the lettuce. And what the hell kind of a salad is that? WATER. You’ve got water.
